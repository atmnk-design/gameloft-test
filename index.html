Task 1: Research & UX Audit
üí™
STRENGTHS

Internal Factors: What has already been done well

  • Mobile-First Approach: On the mobile version, elements function well; it is evident that the design was developed primarily for the mobile version.
  • High-Quality Card UI: Product cards look appealing, with polished micro-interactions implemented on hover.
  • Interactivity: There is a nice "bounce" effect (when scrolling to the end) in the horizontal product scroll.
  • Clean Profile Foundation: The account page is currently simple, without unnecessary elements, serving as a good foundation.
  • Presence of FAQ: There is a separate FAQ page (worth splitting by topics), which will reduce support requests.
üìâ
WEAKNESSES

Structural Problems

  • Missing Navigation: No header listing all games as website pages.
  • The "Home Page" button is duplicated in the footer, although this function should be performed by the logo.

UX (Usability) Issues

  • Horizontal scroll sections lack obviousness (missing cursor change to "hand" and navigation arrows).
  • It is impossible to register directly on the site itself.
  • No ability to fix login issues on the site itself (violation of N/N Group heuristics).
  • The "Info" button on the product card redirects to a page where the item cannot be purchased at all, leading to decreased conversion (instead of retaining the user).
üöÄ
OPPORTUNITIES

Profile Redesign (Single-Page Experience)

  • Improve account structure: place all information on one page with vertical scrolling, but keep anchor navigation for subsections for convenience.

Competitive Advantage (Inventory Sync)

  • Ensure a mechanic where, when a user buys an item and then joins the game (even if not yet registered), they immediately receive items in their inventory. This is an advantage over competitors (Zynga, Scopely, EA, Supercell).

Improved Integration (QR)

  • Implement game account linking to the online store via QR code (as in Empires and Puzzles from Zynga Games), ensuring simultaneous integration of both mobile and desktop versions of the site.

Functional Implementations

  • Cart: Must add a shopping cart.
  • Registration: Add the ability to register via Google/Apple accounts (like EA Sports).
  • Bonuses: Introduce additional rewards for purchasing items on the site.
  • Modal Cards: Make product cards like in Brawl Stars (retain the user in a modal window with full information).
  • Checkout Optimization: Implement dynamic upsell offers directly on the checkout page. Suggest a complementary item at a special price or an upgrade to a more expensive but "better value" bundle/subscription relative to what is currently in the cart.

THREATS

External risks and mitigation strategies

High Friction (High Entry Threshold)

  • User laziness to switch to the site because In-App is more convenient.
    • Solution Aggressive highlighting of benefits ("+20% BONUS") in the Hero Section of the site and promoting it in the game itself (on the product card/page, for example).

Phishing & Security

  • Player distrust of entering card details on "some site".
    • Solution Use of Trust Signals (partner logos) and High Fidelity UI.

Platform Policy Changes

  • Risk of banning direct store advertising within games.
    • Solution Implementation of "Daily Web Reward" to form a habit of visiting the site directly.

Technical Downtime

  • Fear of delivery delays.
    • Solution Moving delivery FAQ to the main page for transparency.
Task 2: Solution-Oriented Thinking
üîë
Weakness 1: High Access Friction

Login Page Dead-end

  • The Issue: While a "Login" button exists, the login page itself lacks a registration option. New users who reach this page expecting to sign up hit a dead end, forcing them to abandon the process.

Proposed Solution: Redesign with Quick Registration

I propose redesigning the login interface to include a clear "Sign Up" toggle or section directly on the login screen.

  • Implementation: Introduce Single Sign-On (SSO) options to allow one-click registration via Google and Apple accounts.
  • Why it works: This eliminates the need for manual form filling and password creation, drastically reducing the time-to-value for new users.
  • Reference: This seamless entry point is best implemented by EA Sports FC Web Store, where account creation is integrated effortlessly into the login flow.
üï∏Ô∏è
Weakness 2: Navigation & Discovery

Navigation & Discovery Dead-ends

  • The Issue: The absence of a global header or game menu prevents users from easily navigating between different game stores, leading to confusion and high bounce rates.

Proposed Solution: "Hub & Spoke" Architecture

I propose transforming the Main Page into a central HUB designed to efficiently route traffic to specific game pages ("Spokes") (as detailed here in Opportunities).

  • Implementation: Develop a persistent Global Navigation Header containing direct links/icons to all 5 games. The body of the page will serve as a gateway, featuring a clear "Game Selector" to guide users to their destination immediately.
  • Why it works: It aligns the user's mental model with industry standards, ensuring they can switch contexts instantly.
  • Reference: This Hub structure is the industry standard for multi-game publishers, successfully implemented by Supercell Store, Scopely, and Zynga Games.
üí≥
Weakness 3: Broken Conversion Path

Product Cards Interaction

  • The Issue: The "Info" button on product cards redirects users to a separate, non-transactional page. This disrupts the purchase flow and increases the likelihood of drop-offs.

Proposed Solution: Modal-Based Product Cards

I propose redesigning the product card interaction to open a Modal View (Pop-up) instead of redirecting to a new page.

  • Implementation: When a user clicks a card, a modal window should appear overlaying the current page. This modal will contain all necessary details (description, bonus info) and, crucially, a direct "Buy" / "Add to Cart" button.
  • Why it works: It keeps the user within the shopping context (The Hub/Spoke) and reduces the interaction cost, making the purchase decision faster and smoother.
  • Reference: This pattern is perfectly executed in the Supercell (Brawl Stars) store, where users can view details and purchase without ever leaving the main listing.
Task 3: Design Leadership Strategy

Context & Approach

Situational Leadership Model

To effectively delegate this task to a Junior Designer, I apply the Situational Leadership Model. Given that a Junior Designer typically falls within the R1 (Enthusiastic Beginner) or R2 (Disillusioned Learner) readiness levels, they require a high degree of directiveness and structured feedback. I implement a combination of S1 (Directing) and S2 (Coaching) leadership styles.

The Toolchain (SSOT)

I execute this approach through an integrated ecosystem, strictly adhering to the Single Source of Truth (SSOT) principle:

  • Notion (Context Layer): Context and Requirements ("Why?").
  • Jira (Process Layer): Process and Deadlines ("When and What?").
  • Figma (Execution Layer): Execution and Feedback ("How?").
üë£
Process Breakdown

1. Explain Objectives (Notion)

  • Context: Explain that players aren't migrating to the web store because in-app feels easier. Goal: "Sell" the value (cheaper, bonuses, official).
  • Requirements: Screen 1 must have a clear benefit message ("+20% Bonus"). Screen 2 must have a clear CTA (Sign Up via Gameloft ID).
  • References: Links to benchmarks (Supercell, EA Sports) to set visual standards.

2. Break Down Steps (Jira)

Large tasks can be overwhelming. I break the assignment into short, manageable milestones:

  • Step 1: Discovery & Low-fi (Days 1-2): Review Notion doc and sketch 3 variations. Goal: Validate logic/copy without spending time on polish.
  • Step 2: Visual Design (Days 3-4): Apply UI using Asphalt assets and Gameloft brand style.
  • Step 3: Final Polish & Hand-off (Day 5): Finalize mockups and prepare states for dev hand-off.

Mentorship & Feedback

"Stranger Review" Methodology

During the feedback phase (Figma), I shift to the S2 (Coaching) style. I ask the designer to view their mockup through the eyes of a "stranger" (e.g., a new developer) who has no context:

  • "Is it clear from the mockup where this button leads without your verbal explanation?"
  • "Is it obvious what screen state this is?"

If the answer is "no," we iterate. This teaches the Junior Designer to create self-sufficient, clear, and documented design solutions, gradually elevating their competence level to R3 (Middle).
